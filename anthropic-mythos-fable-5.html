Anthropic Mythos & Fable 5: A Tale of Two AI Models

Frontier AI systems such as Anthropic’s Mythos and its public-facing version, Fable 5, are no longer being treated as ordinary software. They are increasingly viewed as strategic technologies, closer in significance to semiconductors, cyber tools, or defense systems. Yet unlike physical goods, AI models do not fit neatly into traditional export-control frameworks or supply-chain doctrines. This creates a profound policy dilemma: governments want to restrict access to potentially dangerous capabilities, but overly broad restrictions can also harm allies, businesses, researchers, and the very safety institutions needed to evaluate these systems responsibly.

The reported capabilities of Anthropic Mythos and Fable 5 raise serious questions about the speed at which frontier AI systems are being developed. If systems of this kind become powerful enough to influence cybersecurity, information control, and automated decision-making, the concern is no longer limited to private companies or technical researchers. It becomes a matter of national security. In that context, warnings from government institutions, including the White House, show how seriously policymakers are beginning to view advanced AI development.

The more important question is whether the government’s unprecedented response was driven by the almost godlike capabilities of Anthropic’s frontier models, or by the government’s own growing dependence on them. Writing in The New York Times, Dustin Volz and Julian E. Barnes suggest that the dispute reflects a deeper contradiction: the Trump administration has become increasingly reliant on advanced AI systems for cybersecurity, even as it clashes with one of the leading American AI developers. Their article reports that the N.S.A. temporarily lost access to a powerful Anthropic AI model during the dispute between the administration and the company.

The Economist further reported that, during a June 11 congressional hearing, Senator Mark Warner stated that Gen. Joshua Rudd, head of the N.S.A. and U.S. Cyber Command, told him that Mythos “broke into almost all of our classified systems, not in weeks but in hours.” According to the report, this was not a hostile attack but part of a mutually agreed collaborative exercise under Anthropic’s initiative known as Project Glasswing.

There is an old legend that when the Taj Mahal was completed in 1653 after twenty-two years of extraordinary labor, Mughal emperor Shah Jahan rewarded the chief architect with gold and silver, but then ordered his hand to be cut off so that he could never build anything so magnificent again. Whether historically true or not, the story captures an enduring human anxiety: how does society respond when someone creates something unprecedented?

That question now returns in a new form. In the age of frontier AI, will society reward transformative innovation, regulate it, nationalize it, restrict it, or punish it for becoming too powerful? The Anthropic dispute suggests that the future of AI governance may not be shaped only by technical capability, but also by fear, dependency, geopolitics, and the uneasy relationship between innovation and control.
